## Why this note exists

DePsy is not "another mental health AI startup." It is a 150-year movement's economic and technological infrastructure, cooperatively owned, with $KNOW token and DAO governance, in partnership with Bonfires AI, with named founding co-governors recruited from the most consequential survivor advocates, scholars, journalists, and lawyers in the field: Robert Whitaker, Vesper Moore, Hel Spandler, Rob Wipond, Laura López-Aybar, Kim Witczak, Jim Gottstein, Pranami Tamuli, Karin Jervert, Alan Robinson.

Building the AI substrate that powers this platform requires answering three questions that "build a chatbot" framings never have to answer:

1. **What is the AI architecturally?** ("Trained on what, in what shape, to do what.")
2. **What is the corpus ethically?** ("Whose words go in, on what basis, with what recognition.")
3. **What is the platform politically?** ("Who governs it, who profits from it, how is extraction prevented.")

The DePsy manifesto answers question 3 definitively: $KNOW token + DAO + cooperative ownership. This strategic note answers questions 1 and 2 by grounding the architecture in Levin's Multi-Scale Competency framework and specifying the corpus-build protocols accordingly.

The result is a defensible, peer-reviewable, ethically rigorous corpus architecture that the founding co-governors can recognize as adequate to the work they have spent careers proving.

## Part 1 — DePsy in MCA terms

### The architectural argument

Levin's framework for biological systems applies to mental health with a precision that's almost startling. The translations:

| Levin (biology) | DePsy (mental health) |
|---|---|
| Cell with local competencies | Person with lived experience and survival strategies |
| Tissue (cells coupled by gap junctions) | Peer support circle, Hearing Voices group, healing community |
| Organ (specialized tissue with system function) | Soteria house, peer respite center, Open Dialogue practice |
| Organism (organs coordinated toward whole-body goals) | A flourishing community held in a non-pathologizing infrastructure |
| Bioelectric gap junctions | Narrative, ritual, witness, relationship — the mechanisms by which selves cohere with collectives |
| Cancer (bioelectric disconnection from collective) | Crisis, dissociation, the experience pharmaceutical psychiatry calls "psychotic break" |
| Plasticity foundational | The brain's actual capacity for reorganization that the medical model treats as exception |
| Stress as goal-directedness signal | Suffering as the system's signal that something needs to change — not a disease to be silenced |

### The healing reframe

Levin in BioEssays 2025: "We don't need to micromanage the molecular hardware. We need to talk to the collective intelligence of tissues and organs in their native bioelectric language and remind them of their goals."

Substitute "collective intelligence of selves" for "tissues and organs" and "narrative-cultural-relational language" for "bioelectric language" and you have the DePsy thesis in Levin's exact grammatical structure.

This is more than rhetorical mapping. It is an architectural argument:

- **Pharmaceutical micromanagement of molecular hardware fails** because it bypasses the multi-scale architecture entirely. SSRIs target serotonin without addressing the relational, narrative, cultural, and economic systems within which the suffering arose. The chemical imbalance theory was the marketing of this micromanagement; the science was always weak; the harm was epistemic and structural at once.

- **Peer respite, Hearing Voices Networks, Soteria, and Open Dialogue work** because they restore the cognitive gap junctions — the bioelectric coupling, in Levin's terms — between the person in crisis and the collective. The person isn't treated as a broken cell to be molecularly rewired. They are reconnected to the larger system they had become disconnected from.

- **DePsy is the infrastructure that operates at the correct architectural level** — the level of the collective, the level of distributed memory, the level of multi-scale coordination. The platform is the gap-junction substrate at planetary scale.

### Why this framing matters operationally

Three things change when DePsy is articulated in MCA terms rather than as "decentralized mental health platform":

1. **Patron pitches become peer-reviewable.** Conscious-capitalist patrons who have read McGilchrist, Iain McGilchrist, Carse, Wilber, or Iain (and who fund Levin's lab — Allen Discovery Center has formal partnership infrastructure) recognize the framework instantly. The pitch becomes "we are building the infrastructure that operates at the architectural level mental health treatment has structurally avoided" rather than "we have a different mental health AI."

2. **Founding co-governor recruitment language becomes precise.** Whitaker has spent two decades documenting the structural failures of pharmaceutical micromanagement. Spandler has built the academic case that compassionate care requires structural infrastructure. Gottstein has won legal cases proving forced drugging is unconstitutional when alternatives exist. López-Aybar has framed the epistemic violence of which methodologies count as legitimate. **All of them have been articulating MCA principles in mental health language for years without the MCA name.** Naming the framework gives them a shared architectural grammar they will recognize.

3. **Corpus design becomes constrained.** If the architectural goal is restoring gap-junctional coupling between selves and collectives, the corpus must reflect collective intelligence in dialogue rather than expert pronouncements transcribed. This is a design constraint that helps decide what goes in and what doesn't.

## Part 2 — The corpus as service, not extraction

### The founding question

The DePsy manifesto is unambiguous: "the days of researchers extracting survivor knowledge for academic papers that survivors never see are over."

This means the corpus build cannot replicate the extractive pattern of academic research. It cannot be "we ingest survivor testimony to train our AI." Even if the testimony is publicly available and fair-use, even if no individual survivor objects, the pattern itself is what the manifesto names as the problem.

The corpus must be reframed from extraction to service.

### The service framing

What the corpus does for the movement, before any contributor opts in:

1. **It demonstrates that DePsy launches grounded in the movement's existing public intelligence.** The Mad Pride history, the survivor advocacy archives, the lawyers' depositions, the journalists' investigative records, the academic frameworks of Mad Studies, the Latin American alternative network's published proceedings — all of this is the movement's existing record. Indexing it gives the platform proof-of-seriousness.

2. **It shifts the recruitment conversation.** Rather than approaching founding co-governors with "please contribute to our database," DePsy approaches them with "we have studied your work; we are building infrastructure adequate to it; we are asking you to govern it." The corpus is the basis for the second sentence, not a substitute for the third.

3. **It operationalizes the manifesto's claim of "150 years of building."** Every journal entry from inside an asylum, every protest publication, every Mad Studies paper, every survivor memoir, every healing circle's published reflections — all of it is movement intelligence that DePsy acknowledges as primary, not as anecdote awaiting clinical validation.

4. **It seeds the gap-junction substrate.** In MCA terms, the platform's coordination capacity grows with the richness of the shared substrate. A DePsy AI that doesn't know who Whitaker is, who Hearing Voices Network is, what Open Dialogue practices look like in implementation — that AI cannot serve the movement, because it lacks the gap-junctional state necessary for coupling with the collective intelligence the movement has already produced.

### The contributor question is sovereignty, not access

The manifesto's $KNOW token + DAO architecture answers the contributor question definitively. Every person who contributes lived experience to DePsy generates an ownership stake. The DAO governs platform decisions. Contributors are co-owners from the founding moment, not data subjects to be extracted from.

The corpus question is **separate and prior**. It asks: does DePsy launch grounded in the movement's existing public intelligence, or does it float free of it? Grounded is correct.

The two questions interact: when a founding co-governor (Whitaker, Gottstein, Wipond, et al.) is invited to govern the platform, part of the invitation includes their decision about how their existing public archive is treated by the corpus. Some will say "index everything I have publicly produced." Others will say "index this but not that." Others will say "exclude my work from training data; allow it only as cited reference material." All three are valid. The contributor protocol must accommodate all three.

## Part 3 — The corpus build protocol

### Scope and sources

**Tier 1 — Existing public movement archives (default-index, attribution required):**

- Mad in America full archive (text + podcasts) — Whitaker is a founding co-governor candidate
- Asylum Magazine archive (text) — Spandler is a founding co-governor candidate
- Mad in Puerto Rico (Spanish + English) — López-Aybar is a co-founder
- Locura magazine (Spanish, RedEsfera Latinoamericana) — Robinson is a founding co-governor candidate
- MindFreedom International archive
- Hearing Voices Network published materials (multiple national chapters)
- Soteria documentation (Loren Mosher's published work + replications)
- Open Dialogue training materials and case studies (Finland and replications)
- Spiritual Emergence Network archives (Stan and Christina Grof's published work)
- Mad Studies published papers and the International Journal of Mad Studies
- Woody Matters / FDA hearing transcripts (Witczak's public advocacy record)
- PsychRights case law and legal briefs (Gottstein's published litigation record)
- Your Consent Is Not Required (Wipond, BenBella 2023) — book + Wipond's journalism archive

**Tier 2 — Public talks and interviews (default-index pending creator confirmation):**

- YouTube interviews and conference talks by named founding co-governor candidates
- Podcast appearances on Mad in America podcast, Plus podcasts, etc.
- Documentary footage where speakers have made public consent for their inclusion
- Conference proceedings (Alternatives Conference, ISPS conferences, etc.)

**Tier 3 — Survivor testimony from public archives (sacred handling, attribution + permission):**

- Material from public survivor archives (older ones like Madness Network News require careful provenance review)
- First-person accounts published in Mad in America, Asylum, Mad in Puerto Rico — these are considered already published with consent
- Material whose provenance is unclear is excluded by default; included only if the speaker affirms

**Tier 4 — Excluded categories (never index):**

- Private correspondence of any kind
- Unpublished hospital records, psychiatric notes, or evaluations
- Material from anyone under 18 at time of speaking
- Material identified as having been obtained under coercion or without informed consent
- Material from speakers who have publicly objected to inclusion in any database
- Material with biographical or identifying information about third parties (family members, clinicians named, etc.) without those parties' consent

### Technical pipeline

The pipeline already exists in Synergyworks; this is configuration of the existing tooling:

1. **Source ingestion via Syntax bot** (existing, /home/openclaw/.openclaw/workspace-conductor/divisions/creative/bots/syntax/)
2. **Parsing into markdown wiki** (existing, substack_to_wiki.py and equivalent for other formats)
3. **YouTube/podcast transcription** via TranscriptAPI (paid tier when corpus build commences) or yt-dlp + Whisper (open-source path); see PLAYBOOK--Video-Corpus-Ingestion.md (queued for write in this same session batch)
4. **Chunking and indexing into ChromaDB** as new collection: `depsy_corpus`, separate from `syntony_times_corpus`
5. **Metadata per chunk:** source, author, attribution, tier (1-4), date, URL, license terms if known, contributor consent status (default/pending/confirmed/excluded)
6. **corpus-api.py extension** to expose `depsy_corpus` collection (existing pattern, second collection endpoint)
7. **Future DePsy AI** queries the collection at the architectural level the platform requires
8. **Cross-query capability** so Kerouac can synthesize Storylivingry pieces that engage with DePsy material when relevant, and vice versa (Multi-Corpus Vision pattern)

### Consent infrastructure

This is the harder, more important part. The technical pipeline is straightforward. The consent infrastructure is what distinguishes DePsy from extractive precedents.

**Phase 1 — Pre-launch consent capture (before any Tier 2 or Tier 3 ingestion):**

- Founding co-governor invitations include explicit corpus consent question
- Each invitee receives the source list of their own publicly available material that the corpus build proposes to index
- Each invitee returns a marked-up consent decision: include, exclude, conditional (with conditions specified)
- The decisions are recorded in a CONTRIBUTOR-CONSENT-LEDGER (kept outside the sync boundary per Session 9 standing rule about secrets and consent records never propagating through the ontology)
- Tier 1 archives are indexed regardless of individual consent (because they are organizational archives, not individual property), with individual material flagged for exclusion if a contributor opts out

**Phase 2 — DAO ratification (at platform launch):**

- The CONTRIBUTOR-CONSENT-LEDGER becomes part of the founding governance documents
- The DAO ratifies the consent protocol as one of the platform's first formal acts
- Any future corpus expansion follows the ratified protocol

**Phase 3 — Ongoing contributor protocol (post-launch):**

- New contributors (anyone earning $KNOW tokens by contributing lived experience) consent at point of contribution
- The consent UI distinguishes: "include in corpus for AI training," "include in commons for peer support," "store privately, share at my discretion only"
- Contributors can revoke or modify consent at any time; the DAO operationalizes that

### What the corpus does NOT do

To preempt extraction patterns even at the technical layer:

- **No facial recognition** on video content. Speaker identification is by metadata only.
- **No voiceprint extraction** from podcast audio.
- **No biographical inference** beyond what speakers have made public themselves.
- **No synthetic survivor content generation** ever. The DePsy AI does not impersonate survivor voices.
- **No "what would [survivor X] say" prompts** in the AI interface. The AI cites and synthesizes; it does not ventriloquize.
- **No corpus chunks containing a single individual's testimony are reproduced verbatim above 50 words** in AI output. Citations and paraphrase are the modes; quotation is rare and bounded.
- **No data export from corpus to third parties** without explicit DAO approval.

These constraints are technical implementations of the manifesto's anti-extraction stance.
